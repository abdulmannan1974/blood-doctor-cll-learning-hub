Module 1: Classification & Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis

Chronic B-cell Disorders with Leukaemic Expression

  • CLL / SLL / MBL — the CLL spectrum
  • SMZL — Splenic marginal zone lymphoma
  • HCL — Hairy-cell leukaemia
  • Splenic B-cell lymphoma/leukaemia with prominent nucleoli (historically HCL variant)
  • Non-nodal leukaemic MCL — Mantle cell lymphoma
  • LPL / WM — Lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma / Waldenström
  • SDRPL — Splenic diffuse red pulp small B-cell lymphoma

Diagnostic Sequence

1Confirm persistent lymphocytosis (>5×109/L for ≥3 months)
2Review blood film morphology with experienced haematopathologist
3Confirm clonality and immunophenotype (B or T, κ/λ)
4Add targeted genetics/FISH where differential exists
5Use tissue biopsy selectively (lymph node, marrow, spleen)

CLL Diagnostic Algorithm

Bloodü©∏Doctor · Dr Abdul Mannan FRCPath FCPS
Clinical Pearl
In typical CLL with high lymphocyte count and classic phenotype, lymph node biopsy is NOT required. Reserve biopsy for atypical cases and suspected lymphoma.
Diagnostic Checklist — CLL Workup:
  • FBC with differential — persistent lymphocytosis ≥3 months
  • Blood film review with experienced haematopathologist
  • Flow cytometry: CD5, CD19, CD20, CD23, CD200, SmIg, FMC7
  • CLL score calculated (4–5 supports CLL)
  • Serum immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM)
  • DAT (baseline, before treatment)
  • Beta-2 microglobulin, LDH
Test Yourself — Module 1
A patient has 4.2 × 109/L clonal B cells with CLL phenotype (score 5/5), no lymphadenopathy, no organomegaly. Diagnosis?
Reveal AnswerMonoclonal B-cell Lymphocytosis (MBL). Clonal B cells <5 × 109/L with CLL phenotype but no tissue disease = MBL. High-count MBL progresses at ~1–2% per year.
What CLL score marker distinguishes CLL from MCL when both are CD5+?
Reveal AnswerCD23 and CD200. CLL is CD23+/CD200+. MCL is CD23−/CD200−. Also check Cyclin D1 and t(11;14) for MCL.

Module 2: CLL Core Concepts

Core

Definition & CLL-SLL Continuum

  • CLL: ≥5×109/L monoclonal B cells in blood for ≥3 months with typical immunophenotype (SmIgweak, CD5+, CD19+, CD20weak, CD23+, CD200+)
  • SLL: tissue-dominant disease (same phenotype) with blood B cells <5×109/L
  • MBL: <5×109/L clonal B cells, no adenopathy/organomegaly/symptoms
  • ~90% present as CLL, ~10% as SLL

Epidemiology & Clinical Features

  • Most frequent leukaemia in Western countries (~34% of all leukaemias)
  • M:F ratio 1.5–2:1; median age at diagnosis ~70 years
  • ~80% diagnosed while asymptomatic (incidental finding)
  • Painless generalised lymphadenopathy (neck, axillae, inguinal)
  • Familial risk: 3–8x higher in first-degree relatives
  • Rare in Asian populations; remains rare in migrant populations

Immune Dysfunction Profile

  • Hypogammaglobulinaemia: affects ~25%, IgM falls first, then IgG, then IgA
  • T-cell exhaustion: inverted CD4/CD8 ratio, increased PD-1/LAG-3/CTLA-4
  • NK cell / ADCC defects
  • Monocyte and granulocyte dysfunction
  • Suboptimal vaccination responses

Laboratory Hallmarks

  • Elevated WBC with absolute lymphocytosis
  • Small mature lymphocytes with scanty cytoplasm
  • Smudge cells (Gumprecht cells) on blood film
  • Prolymphocytes 1–10% nearly always present when WBC >30×109/L
  • Prolymphocytes >15% = accelerated/transforming CLL
High Yield
High WBC count alone does NOT mandate treatment in CLL. Treatment is only indicated for active/progressive disease.
Test Yourself — Module 2
A CLL patient has prolymphocytes >15% on blood film. What does this signify?
Reveal AnswerAccelerated / transforming CLL. Prolymphocytes >15% (formerly called “prolymphocytic transformation”) signals disease acceleration. Assess for Richter transformation.
Which immunoglobulin class falls first in CLL-related hypogammaglobulinaemia?
Reveal AnswerIgM falls first, then IgG, then IgA. Hypogammaglobulinaemia affects ~25% and is the major driver of infection susceptibility.

Module 3: Molecular & Cytogenetics Simplified

High Priority

IGHV Mutated vs Unmutated

Two biologic behaviours in one disease. Mutated IGHV (<98% germline homology) = post-germinal centre origin, indolent biology, longer treatment-free interval, median survival >15 years. Unmutated IGHV = pre-GC origin, aggressive kinetics, shorter survival (6–7 years historically).

del(13q) Isolated

Generally favourable biology. Present in 25–40% of cases. Associated with mutated IGHV, MYD88 mutations, miR-15a/miR-16 involvement. Median survival >15 years when found as sole abnormality.

del(11q) / ATM

Higher-risk profile in CIT era. Present in 20–25%. Associated with unmutated IGHV, ATM mutations (30%), BIRC3 mutations. Historically shorter treatment-free survival. BCRi/BCL2i overcome poor prognostic significance.

del(17p) / TP53

Poor CIT response; pathway inhibitor preference. Present in 5–50% depending on disease stage. Usually accompanied by TP53 mutation on other allele. The most important predictor of CIT resistance. Must use BTKi or BCL2i. Median survival 3–6 years with CIT.

Complex Karyotype

Risk escalation marker. Defined as >3 or >5 aberrations. Found in 5–30%. Often includes TP53 mutations. Associated with clonal evolution and poorer outcomes even with targeted therapy.

BCR Signalling & Apoptosis Escape

Why BTKi and BCL2i work. CLL cells depend on BCR signalling for survival and proliferation → BTK inhibitors block this pathway. CLL cells overexpress BCL2 (antiapoptotic) → venetoclax directly triggers apoptosis. This explains the class-switch strategy in relapsed disease.

Think of IGHV status as the master switch: mutated = slow lane, unmutated = fast lane. TP53/del(17p) = CIT exit ramp → must use BTKi or BCL2i.
Pre-Treatment Molecular Workup Checklist:
  • FISH panel: del(13q), trisomy 12, del(11q), del(17p)
  • TP53 mutation status by NGS (mandatory before any therapy)
  • IGHV mutation status
  • Complex karyotype assessment (stimulated metaphase)
Test Yourself — Module 3
A CLL patient has del(17p) on FISH and TP53 mutation on NGS. They are young and fit. Can you give them FCR?
Reveal AnswerNo. TP53/del(17p) disruption predicts CIT resistance. Must use pathway inhibitors: BTKi (zanubrutinib/acalabrutinib) or venetoclax + anti-CD20. FCR will produce short-lived, poor-quality responses.
Why do CLL cells overexpress BCL2 and why does this matter therapeutically?
Reveal AnswerBCL2 is an anti-apoptotic protein. CLL cells depend on it for survival. Venetoclax is a BH3-mimetic that directly inhibits BCL2, triggering apoptosis. This explains why it works even in TP53-disrupted disease (apoptosis independent of p53).

StageFeaturesMedian Survival

Good-Risk Pattern

  • Early stage (Binet A, Rai 0)
  • Mutated IGHV
  • Isolated del(13q)
  • Normal beta-2 microglobulin
  • Low blood lymphocyte count (<15×109/L)
  • Long LDT (>12 months)

High-Risk Pattern

  • Advanced stage (Binet B/C, Rai I–IV)
  • Unmutated IGHV
  • del(17p)/TP53, del(11q), complex karyotype
  • Elevated beta-2 microglobulin
  • High blood lymphocyte count (≥15×109/L)
  • Short LDT (<12 months)
Clinical Pearl
The CLL-IPI combines Rai stage, age, TP53, IGHV status and beta-2 microglobulin into four risk groups. However, it was developed for CIT-treated patients and poorly predicts outcomes on BTKi/BCL2i.
Test Yourself — Module 4
A patient has Hb 95 g/L from marrow failure and platelets 85 × 109/L. What is their Rai stage?
Reveal AnswerRai Stage IV (High Risk). Stage III = anaemia (Hb <110 g/L) from marrow failure. Stage IV = thrombocytopenia (<100 × 109/L). Having both makes it Stage IV. Median survival 3–5 years historically.
Patient has Binet A with mutated IGHV, isolated del(13q), normal beta-2 microglobulin. Prognosis?
Reveal AnswerExcellent prognosis. All favourable markers: early stage, mutated IGHV, isolated del(13q), normal beta-2M. Median survival >15 years. Many patients never require treatment.

Module 5: Treatment Strategy

Treatment

When NOT to Treat: Active Surveillance

Asymptomatic early-stage CLL (Binet A, Rai 0) without active disease features should be observed with periodic monitoring. Early treatment has NOT shown survival benefit.

Treatment Initiation Criteria

  • Hb <110 g/L from marrow failure
  • Platelets <100×109/L from marrow failure
  • Massive (≥6 cm below costal margin) or progressive splenomegaly
  • Massive (≥10 cm) or progressive lymphadenopathy
  • Progressive lymphocytosis (>50% increase in 2 months or LDT <6 months)
  • Constitutional symptoms (B symptoms)
  • Refractory autoimmune cytopenia

Treatment Initiation Checklist:

  • TP53/del(17p) status known before starting any therapy
  • IGHV mutation status available
  • Hepatitis B screening (HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc) before anti-CD20
  • Baseline cardiac assessment if considering BTKi (ECG, BP, AF history)
  • TLS risk assessment if considering venetoclax (tumour burden, renal function, urate)
  • Patient fitness/frailty assessed (CIRS, ECOG)
Test Yourself — Module 5
A 58-year-old fit woman with CLL (mutated IGHV, no TP53, Binet C) needs treatment. What is the one regimen with curative potential in this subgroup?
Reveal AnswerFCR (Fludarabine, Cyclophosphamide, Rituximab). In fit patients with mutated IGHV and no TP53, FCR achieves long-term (60%+) remissions >10 years. This is the only CLL context where CIT has curative potential.
Patient on ibrutinib develops AF. What two drug classes should you AVOID combining with ibrutinib?
Reveal AnswerWarfarin (bleeding risk with ibrutinib platelet dysfunction) and verapamil/diltiazem (CYP3A4 inhibitors increase ibrutinib levels). Use DOACs cautiously; prefer beta-blockers for rate control.

Module 6: Complications & Supportive Care

Complications

Autoimmune Cytopenias

  • AIHA: ~10% of CLL patients; elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, positive DAT (IgG/C3)
  • ITP: 5–7%; isolated thrombocytopenia with preserved marrow megakaryocytes
  • Evans syndrome: concurrent AIHA + ITP
  • PRCA: <1%; low reticulocytes, arrested red cell precursors
  • Treatment: corticosteroids first → rituximab → alternative immunosuppression → anti-CLL therapy if refractory

Infection Risk & Vaccination

  • Infections are the commonest cause of death (40–60%)
  • Multifactorial: low IgG, impaired antigen presentation, treatment toxicity
  • IgG replacement for profound hypogammaglobulinaemia + recurrent infections
  • Screen ALL patients for hepatitis B before anti-CD20 therapy
  • Vaccination: inactivated influenza, pneumococcal, herpes zoster — response is suboptimal but still recommended

Richter Transformation

  • Occurs in ~5% of patients; transformation to DLBCL (most common) or Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Clonally related (80%): poor prognosis, median survival <1 year; consider allo-HSCT
  • Clonally unrelated (20%): prognosis like de novo DLBCL; treat with R-CHOP
  • Associated with: unmutated IGHV, NOTCH1, TP53, CDKN2A/B, MYC mutations
  • PET/CT helps target biopsy to metabolically active sites

Second Neoplasias

  • 10–15% of CLL patients develop other cancers
  • Melanoma, lung carcinoma, lymphoma, GI tumours most frequent
  • Substantially increased risk of Merkel cell carcinoma
  • Secondary MDS/AML in ~5% of heavily pre-treated patients (e.g., post-FCR)

Richter Transformation Workup Algorithm

Bloodü©∏Doctor · Dr Abdul Mannan FRCPath FCPS
Red Flag
Rapid node growth + rising LDH + B symptoms → suspect Richter transformation. Urgent tissue biopsy from the most metabolically active site (PET-guided) is essential.
Supportive Care Checklist — All CLL Patients:
  • Immunoglobulin levels checked regularly; IgG replacement if recurrent infections + low IgG
  • Vaccination: inactivated influenza, pneumococcal, herpes zoster (Shingrix)
  • Hepatitis B screen before ANY anti-CD20 therapy
  • Aciclovir prophylaxis during purine analogue / venetoclax therapy
  • Annual skin surveillance (Merkel cell carcinoma, melanoma risk)
  • DAT monitoring for autoimmune cytopenias
Test Yourself — Module 6
A CLL patient has sudden Hb drop to 68 g/L with reticulocytes 10%, LDH 800, haptoglobin undetectable, DAT positive. Platelets also 45 × 109/L with preserved megakaryocytes. Diagnosis?
Reveal AnswerEvans Syndrome (concurrent AIHA + ITP). Warm AIHA (DAT positive with haemolytic profile) PLUS immune thrombocytopenia (low platelets, preserved megakaryocytes). First-line: corticosteroids. Escalate to rituximab, then anti-CLL therapy if refractory.
Richter transformation biopsy shows clonally related DLBCL. What is the approximate median overall survival?
Reveal Answer<1 year. Clonally related (80% of cases) has very poor prognosis. R-CHOP/R-EPOCH → allo-HSCT if eligible. Clonally unrelated (20%) has much better outcomes, treated like de novo DLBCL.

Module 7: Other Chronic B-cell Disorders

Differential

Comprehensive guide to CLL mimics and other chronic B-cell lymphoproliferative disorders with leukaemic expression. Each disorder has actionable diagnostic checklists, treatment pathways, and key differentiators.

SMZL — Splenic Marginal Zone Lymphoma

Click to expand
Clinical Features
  • Median age ~70 years; slight M:F predominance
  • Massive splenomegaly — often >6 cm below costal margin
  • Villous lymphocytes on blood film (short polar villi)
  • Moderate lymphocytosis (often 10–30 × 109/L)
  • Autoimmune cytopenias in ~15%
  • Hepatitis C association (screen all patients)
  • Minimal or no peripheral lymphadenopathy
Immunophenotype & Genetics
  • CD19+, CD20+, CD11c+
  • CD5−, CD23−, CD103−, CD123−, CD25−
  • No BRAF V600E
  • NOTCH2 mutations (10–25%)
  • KLF2 mutations (~20%)
  • del(7q22-32) in ~30% — most frequent cytogenetic abnormality
  • BM: intrasinusoidal infiltration pattern (characteristic)
Treatment Pathway: HCV+ → treat hepatitis first (may resolve lymphoma). HCV− → Rituximab monotherapy is standard first-line. Splenectomy reserved for refractory cytopenias or diagnostic uncertainty. OS 10–15+ years.
  • Villous lymphocytes on film
  • Splenomegaly confirmed
  • CD103−, CD25−, BRAF− (excludes HCL)
  • Intrasinusoidal BM pattern
  • Hepatitis C screen done

HCL — Hairy-Cell Leukaemia

Click to expand
Clinical Presentation
  • 2% of all leukaemias; M:F ratio 4:1
  • Median age 55 years
  • Pancytopenia with monocytopenia (hallmark)
  • Moderate splenomegaly; minimal lymphadenopathy
  • Hairy cells: pale-grey abundant cytoplasm with fine projections
  • BM aspirate: dry tap (fibrosis)
  • BM trephine: clear-zone “fried-egg” infiltration pattern
Immunophenotype & Genetics
  • CD19+, CD20 bright, CD11c+
  • CD103+, CD123+, CD25+, Annexin A1+
  • CD5−, CD23−
  • BRAF V600E in virtually 100% (pathognomonic)
  • IGHV mutated in most cases (usually VH4-34−)
  • TRAP (tartrate-resistant acid phosphatase) positive
First-Line Treatment
  • Cladribine (2-CdA) single course — CR 70–90%, ORR >95%
  • Pentostatin alternative — similar efficacy
  • Median remission duration: 15 years
  • Pre-treatment: G-CSF if neutropenia, aciclovir prophylaxis
Relapsed Disease
  • Vemurafenib + Rituximab — CR >85%, MRD negativity
  • Repeat purine analogue if remission >5 years
  • Moxetumomab pasudotox (anti-CD22 immunotoxin)
  • Ibrutinib shows activity (off-label)
Diagnostic Pearl: If pancytopenia + monocytopenia + dry tap + BRAF V600E = Classical HCL until proven otherwise. Annexin A1 is the most specific marker (absent in all mimics).
  • Pancytopenia with monocytopenia
  • Hairy cell morphology on film
  • CD103+, CD123+, CD25+, Annexin A1+
  • BRAF V600E confirmed
  • BM trephine: clear-zone pattern
  • Pre-treatment infection prophylaxis planned

SBCL-PN — Splenic B-cell Lymphoma/Leukaemia with Prominent Nucleoli

HCL Variant · Click to expand
Key Differences from Classical HCL
  • WHO 5th edition reclassified from “HCL variant”
  • NO BRAF V600E (critical distinction)
  • NO monocytopenia (unlike classical HCL)
  • Usually leucocytosis (not pancytopenia)
  • Cells with prominent nucleoli + hairy projections
  • Normal MCV (HCL often has raised MCV)
Immunophenotype & Genetics
  • CD11c+, CD103 ±, CD123+
  • CD25−, Annexin A1−
  • MAP2K1 mutations common (~40%)
  • IGHV unmutated in many cases
  • Often VH4-34 usage (unlike classical HCL)
Treatment: Responds poorly to purine analogues alone. Cladribine + Rituximab combination achieves better responses. Trametinib (MEK inhibitor) for MAP2K1-mutated cases. Splenectomy in selected refractory patients.
Pitfall
Do NOT assume “hairy cells = HCL.” If CD25− and BRAF−, this is SBCL-PN and requires different treatment. Purine analogues alone are insufficient.

Non-nodal Leukaemic Mantle Cell Lymphoma

Indolent Subtype · Click to expand
Clinical Features
  • Leukaemic presentation without significant lymphadenopathy
  • Splenomegaly may be present
  • Can mimic CLL morphologically
  • Typically more indolent course than nodal MCL
  • Median age ~65 years
  • Some patients can be observed (watch & wait)
Immunophenotype & Genetics
  • Cyclin D1+, t(11;14)(q13;q32) — mandatory for diagnosis
  • SOX11− (vs SOX11+ in classical nodal MCL)
  • CD5+, CD200−, CD23−
  • IGHV mutated in many cases
  • No TP53 disruption (if present → aggressive behaviour)
Management: If SOX11−, mutated IGHV, no TP53 → can watch & wait. If treatment needed: BTKi or bendamustine-rituximab. Transform to blastoid/pleomorphic MCL requires aggressive therapy.

Follicular Lymphoma — Leukaemic Phase

Click to expand
Clinical Features
  • ~15% of FL patients have circulating cells
  • Cleaved nuclei (centrocytes) with angular cleft — characteristic on film
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy is typical
  • BM: paratrabecular infiltration (unlike CLL which is interstitial/diffuse)
Immunophenotype & Genetics
  • CD10+, BCL6+, BCL2+
  • CD5−, CD23− or weak
  • t(14;18)(q32;q21) — BCL2 rearrangement
  • Surface Ig bright (unlike CLL)
Key Discriminator: CD10 positivity + t(14;18) + paratrabecular marrow pattern = FL, not CLL. Treatment follows standard FL guidelines (rituximab ± chemotherapy, obinutuzumab-based regimens).

LPL / Waldenström Macroglobulinaemia

Click to expand
Clinical Features
  • Small lymphocytes + plasmacytoid cells + plasma cells
  • Monoclonal IgM paraprotein (hallmark of WM)
  • Hyperviscosity symptoms in ~15% (blurred vision, headache, bleeding)
  • Cold agglutinin disease, cryoglobulinaemia, peripheral neuropathy
  • Hepatosplenomegaly; lymphadenopathy less prominent
Immunophenotype & Genetics
  • CD19+, CD20+, CD138+ (plasma cell component)
  • CD5− or weak, CD10−, CD23−
  • MYD88 L265P in >90% (near-pathognomonic)
  • CXCR4 mutations in ~30% (affects treatment response)
Treatment: Hyperviscosity → urgent plasmapheresis. First-line: BTKi (zanubrutinib preferred) or BR (bendamustine-rituximab). CXCR4-mutated cases respond less well to BTKi. Avoid rituximab monotherapy upfront if IgM >40 g/L (IgM flare risk).
  • Monoclonal IgM paraprotein detected
  • Lymphoplasmacytic morphology on film/BM
  • MYD88 L265P tested/confirmed
  • CXCR4 mutation status checked
  • Viscosity level measured (if symptoms)

SDRPL — Splenic Diffuse Red Pulp Small B-cell Lymphoma

Rare · Click to expand
Key Features
  • Rare; involves splenic red pulp diffusely (not marginal zone)
  • Villous lymphocytes with round nuclei (unlike SMZL polar villi)
  • Moderate splenomegaly
  • CD5−, CD23−, Annexin A1−
  • CD11c+, CD103+, CD123+, CD25−
Genetics & Prognosis
  • CCND3 mutations (Cyclin D3) — characteristic
  • Cyclin D3+ by immunohistochemistry
  • No BRAF V600E (excludes HCL)
  • Indolent course; splenectomy may be diagnostic and therapeutic
  • Limited data: rituximab-based therapy if progressive

Test Yourself — Module 7 Quick Check
A patient has pancytopenia, monocytopenia, dry tap, and cells that are CD103+, CD25+, Annexin A1+. What single molecular test clinches the diagnosis?
Reveal AnswerBRAF V600E mutation. Present in virtually 100% of classical HCL. Negative in all mimics (SBCL-PN, SMZL, SDRPL).
Which disorder has intrasinusoidal BM infiltration, NOTCH2 mutations, and an association with hepatitis C?
Reveal AnswerSMZL (Splenic Marginal Zone Lymphoma). Always screen for HCV — treating hepatitis may resolve the lymphoma.
A patient with t(11;14), Cyclin D1+, SOX11−, and no lymphadenopathy. Diagnosis and management approach?
Reveal AnswerNon-nodal leukaemic MCL. SOX11− with no nodes = indolent subtype. Can be observed if no TP53 disruption and mutated IGHV.
